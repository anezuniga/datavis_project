History of Zoning in the MAPC Region
1999
MassGIS
MassGIS
The history of zoning in the MAPC region is marked by a journey towards greater transparency, accessibility, and regional collaboration. With the last comprehensive effort to compile zoning data dating back to 1999 by MassGIS, the need for updated and standardized information became increasingly apparent.
Zoning Atlas Initiation
2011
Zoning Atlas Initiation
In response, MAPC initiated the Zoning Atlas project in 2011, recognizing the significance of having current and comprehensive zoning data for informed decision-making and planning processes. However, the undertaking was not without its challenges, including municipal nonresponse and the complexities involved in spatial boundary delineation and bylaw interpretation. Despite these obstacles, MAPC remained committed to the project, driven by the understanding that a comprehensive regional zoning database could serve as a valuable resource for municipalities, stakeholders, and researchers alike.
2020
Zoning Atlas Launch
Zoning Atlas Launch
Over the years, the Zoning Atlas evolved from a concept into a tangible resource, thanks to collaborative efforts and input from an advisory group comprising municipal planners, state agency staff, and other stakeholders. This collaborative approach ensured that the Atlas reflected the diverse needs and perspectives of the region's communities. The launch of the Zoning Atlas in December 2020 marked a significant milestone in the region's zoning history, providing a foundational dataset that can be continually updated and improved over time. As a dynamic and evolving resource, the Zoning Atlas embodies MAPC's commitment to promoting transparent and equitable governance while empowering municipalities to make informed decisions that shape the future of the MAPC region.

Exclusionary Zoning?
Exclusionary zoning laws, rooted in historical discrimination and perpetuated through indirect methods, have long been a tool of social exclusion in housing markets. Originating in concerns about fire hazards and light-and-air regulations, these laws evolved to maintain property prices and segregate communities. Despite explicit race-based zoning being banned, indirect methods continued to segregate neighborhoods, disproportionately affecting residents of color, particularly Black residents, lower-income and working-class individuals, families with school-aged children, religious minorities, and immigrants. Such zoning practices favor large, costly, single-family residences, rendering them unaffordable for many, exacerbating housing shortages, and perpetuating socioeconomic disparities. Reform efforts, such as the American Jobs Plan's Unlocking Possibilities Program, aim to dismantle these barriers, promote inclusivity, and address the deep-rooted inequities in the housing market. Additionally, initiatives like "living little" and "living denser" advocate for diverse housing typologies and zoning reforms to create more affordable and inclusive communities.
